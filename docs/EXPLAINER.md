# LeaseGuard — Plain English Explainer

> What it does, who it's for, and why it matters.

---

## The Problem

When you rent an apartment, you sign a legal contract that was written by a lawyer
hired by your landlord. That contract is designed to protect the landlord. It is
typically 20 to 50 pages long, written in dense legal language, and you are expected
to sign it before moving in — often under time pressure.

Most tenants do one of two things:

1. Skim it and sign anyway
2. Read it, not fully understand it, and sign anyway

The result is that tenants routinely agree to clauses that are aggressive, unusual,
or in some cases unenforceable under Ontario law — without ever knowing it. Landlords count on this.

---

## What LeaseGuard Does

You upload your lease as a PDF. LeaseGuard reads the entire document, breaks it into
individual clauses, looks up what Ontario law actually says about each one, and tells
you — in plain English — what you are agreeing to, what is risky, what is missing,
and what you can push back on.

It takes roughly 60 to 90 seconds. You get a full report.

---

## How It Works, Step by Step

**Step 1 — You upload your lease PDF**  
Drag and drop your lease. LeaseGuard handles the rest. It works with standard digital
leases and scanned paper leases.

**Step 2 — The agent reads and organises the document**  
LeaseGuard figures out which province and city your lease covers (so it knows which
laws apply), then splits the document into every individual clause — the way a lawyer
would read it, not as one long block of text.

**Step 3 — It looks up real law for each clause**  
For each clause that matters, LeaseGuard searches the actual text of Ontario's
Residential Tenancies Act and real rulings from the Landlord and Tenant Board. It does
not guess or rely on what an AI was trained to believe — it reads the law the same way
your lawyer would, just much faster. Every legal claim in your report links back to
the exact statute section or case it came from.

**Step 4 — It finds problems you would not notice**  
LeaseGuard looks for three types of issues most tenants miss:

- **Risky clauses** — things that are in the lease that you should be worried about,
  such as a clause letting the landlord raise rent by any amount with 30 days notice,
  or a clause making you responsible for repairs the landlord is legally required to
  handle
- **Contradictions** — two clauses in the same lease that conflict with each other,
  which creates legal ambiguity that usually hurts the tenant
- **Missing protections** — rights you are entitled to under Ontario law that are not
  in your lease at all. Many tenants do not know that they have rights that apply
  regardless of what the lease says — LeaseGuard tells you what those are

**Step 5 — It tells you what to do about it**  
For every risky clause, LeaseGuard gives you a negotiation guide: what to ask your
landlord to change, the exact replacement wording to propose, the legal reason they
should accept it, what they might say back, and how to respond. It tells you which
clauses are worth fighting for and which ones are standard boilerplate not worth
your time.

**Step 6 — You get a report you can act on**  
The final report gives you an overall risk score, a plain-English summary, every
problem ranked by severity, and a negotiation guide prioritised by what matters most.
Every claim in the report links to its source — the actual law or tribunal ruling
behind it.

## What the Report Looks Like

**Overview**
> Overall Risk Score: 7.4 / 10 — High  
> "This lease contains several clauses that deviate significantly from Ontario's
> standard protections. Three clauses may conflict with the Residential Tenancies
> Act. Two clauses contradict each other. Four standard tenant protections are
> absent."

**Red Flags** *(clauses that need immediate attention)*
> **Clause 14 — Early Termination Fee**  
> Risk: 8.5 / 10  
> What it says: You owe three months rent if you break the lease early for any reason.  
> What the law says: Under Section 88 of the RTA, a landlord's remedy for early
> termination is limited to actual losses, not a fixed penalty. This clause likely
> cannot be enforced as written.  
> What to do: Ask the landlord to remove this clause entirely. If they refuse,
> that is a strong signal to walk away.

**Missing Protections**
> **Your landlord is legally required to give 24 hours written notice before
> entering your unit.** This is guaranteed by Section 27 of the Residential
> Tenancies Act regardless of what your lease says — but your lease does not
> mention it, which means you may not know to enforce it.

**Negotiation Guide**
> **Clause 8 — Landlord Entry Rights** *(Priority: High)*  
> Ask for: Change "reasonable notice" to "at least 24 hours written notice"  
> Proposed wording: "The landlord will provide at least 24 hours written notice
> before entering the unit except in the case of emergency, in accordance with
> Section 27 of the Residential Tenancies Act."  
> Legal basis: Section 27, RTA — landlords are required by law to provide this
> notice. They have no legal reason to resist this change.  
> If they say: "That's just standard language."  
> You say: "The law requires 24 hours written notice specifically. I'd like the
> lease to reflect what the law already requires."

## Who It Is For

**Students and first-time renters**  
Most people signing their first lease have no idea what is standard and what is not.
LeaseGuard levels that playing field without requiring a law degree or a $300 lawyer
consultation.

**Anyone signing a lease under time pressure**  
Rental markets move fast. Landlords often give 24 to 48 hours to sign. LeaseGuard
turns a 90-minute manual read into a 90-second analysis so you can make an informed
decision without losing the apartment.

**International students and newcomers**  
Tenant rights vary enormously between countries. Someone moving to Ontario from
abroad may assume their lease is standard when it is not. LeaseGuard translates the
legal landscape for people who are unfamiliar with it.

**Anyone who has ever signed something they did not fully understand**  
Which is most people.

## What Makes It Different

There are other tools that let you "chat with your document" or ask an AI questions
about a PDF. LeaseGuard is not that.

The difference is evidence. When LeaseGuard says a clause is risky, it shows you the
exact section of the law it is measuring against and the exact tribunal ruling that
backs up its reasoning. You can click through to the source. You can verify it
yourself. It is not an AI's opinion — it is the law, retrieved and applied to your
specific lease.

A chatbot tells you what it thinks. LeaseGuard shows you what the law says.

---

## What It Does Not Do

LeaseGuard is a research and analysis tool. It is not a lawyer.

- It does not give legal advice and explicitly says so on every report
- It does not cover commercial leases
- It currently covers Ontario only (expanding to other provinces in future)
- It cannot predict how a specific judge or adjudicator would rule on a dispute
- It cannot negotiate on your behalf

For anything that requires professional legal judgment — especially if you are
already in a dispute with your landlord — consult a licensed paralegal or lawyer.
Community Legal Clinics in Ontario offer free legal help for tenants.
