ONTARIO LANDLORD AND TENANT BOARD
File Number: TNT-40004-22

BETWEEN:
Applicant Tenant
and
Respondent Landlord

REASONS FOR ORDER

BACKGROUND
The Tenant applied after the Landlord threatened to terminate the tenancy because the Tenant acquired a pet after the tenancy began, asserting that the Tenant's agreement at the start of the tenancy to a no-pets policy constituted a binding waiver of section 14 rights.

ANALYSIS
The tenancy agreement stated: "The Tenant acknowledges the no-pets policy of this building and voluntarily agrees not to keep any pets in the unit during the tenancy. The Tenant waives any rights under section 14 of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 with respect to this agreement."

The express reference to section 14 in the waiver does not save it. Section 3 of the Act provides that any agreement to waive rights under the Act is void. The inclusion of the phrase "voluntarily agrees" or "waives any rights under section 14" cannot override the Act's own prohibition on contracting out.

Section 14 voids no-pet clauses by operation of law. It does not create a waivable right — it creates a prohibition. There is nothing to waive because the clause itself has no legal effect from the moment it is written.

The Board rejects the Landlord's argument that allowing a waiver gives sophisticated tenants more autonomy. The Act's protections apply uniformly and cannot be traded away individually, as this would enable landlords to obtain such waivers as a precondition of tenancy in all cases.

PRINCIPLE
A tenant cannot waive the protection of section 14 of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006. A purported waiver of the right to keep pets, even if labelled voluntary or expressly referencing section 14, is void under section 3 of the Act. No-pet clauses are void regardless of whether a tenant signed an agreement acknowledging them.

ORDER
The Board declares the no-pets clause and the purported waiver both void and of no effect. The notice of termination is quashed.
