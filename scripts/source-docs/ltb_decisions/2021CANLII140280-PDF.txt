G.R. Feldman Investments Ltd. (the 'Landlord') applied for an order to terminate the tenancy and
evict Kelly Smith-Oliver (the 'Tenant') The Landlord also claimed compensation for each day the
Tenant remained in the unit after the termination date.
This application was heard by videoconference on August 10, 2021.
The Landlord’s legal representative Geoff Paine attended the hearing. The Landlord’s witnesses
Leah Petutin and Rocco Morra also attended.
The Tenant was not present or represented by 11:51 p.m. although properly served with notice of
the hearing by the Board.
Determinations:
1. This application is based upon a second N5 Notice of Termination given under
subsection 68(1) of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the ‘Act’), which provides:
68 (1) A landlord may give a tenant notice of termination of the tenancy if,
(a) a notice of termination was given to the tenant under section 62, 64 or
67; and
(b) more than seven days but less than six months after the notice
mentioned in clause (a) was given to the tenant, an activity takes place,
conduct occurs or a situation arises that constitutes grounds for a notice of
termination under section 60, 61, 62, 64 or 67, other than an activity, conduct
or a situation that is described in subsection 61 (1) and that involves an
illegal act, trade, business or occupation described in clause 61 (2) (a).
2. In this case, the Landlord gave the Tenants two N5 Notices of Termination pursuant to
subsection 64(1) of the Act, which provides:

64 (1) A landlord may give a tenant notice of termination of the tenancy if the
conduct of the tenant, another occupant of the rental unit or a person permitted in
the residential complex by the tenant is such that it substantially interferes with the
reasonable enjoyment of the residential complex for all usual purposes by the
landlord or another tenant or substantially interferes with another lawful right,
privilege or interest of the landlord or another tenant.
3. The Landlord gave the first voidable N5 to the Tenant on January 18, 2021. The voiding
period for this notice is January 19-25, 2021. This notice concerns allegations of cigarette
smoke disturbing neighbouring tenants in 2021 on January 6, January 7, January 8,
January 9 and January 11.
4. The Landlord’s witness, Leah Petutin, who is the Tenant’s neighbour testified that she
wrote complaints to the Landlord about the Tenant’s smoking. Copies of these complaints
were introduced into evidence. Ms. Petutin confirmed that the Tenant’s smoking disturbed
her on each of the dates particularized on the first N5, that the cigarette smoke generally
filled her unit and triggered her asthma. This uncontested evidence persuaded me that
the Landlord was entitled to give the first voidable N5 and therefore the second nonvoidable N5.
5. The Landlord’s second non-voidable N5 was given to the Tenant on February 11, 2021.
This N5 alleges that on February 2, 2021 the Tenant’s cigarette smoking disturbed her
neighbour and that this amounts to a substantial interference within the meaning of the
Act. As this incident occurred more than seven days but less than six months after the
first voidable N5 was given to the Tenant, the Landlord was entitled to give the second
N5 and to apply to the Board after giving this notice.
6. The Landlord proved on the balance or probabilities that the Tenant substantially
interfered with another tenant’s reasonable enjoyment of the residential complex for all
usual purposes. I reach this conclusion after accepting the Landlord’s uncontested
evidence. In particular, Ms. Petutin testified that she suffers from chronic asthma and that
when the Tenant smokes the smoke on her balcony the smoke enters her unit and puts
her into an asthmatic fit for which she has to use her Ventolin aerochamber. She
introduced a letter of complaint she sent to the Landlord on February 2, 2021 confirming
that the Tenant was smoking on February 2, 2021. She confirmed that she could see the
Tenant smoking on her balcony and that the smoking interfered with her ability to
breathe.

8. The Landlord’s witness Mr. Rocco Mora testified that he works at the residential complex
and that he has investigated the odour of smoke emanating from the Tenant’s unit. He
testified that he himself has experienced the odour of smoke and that he has also had
conversations with the Tenant about the smoke in which the Tenant promised to stop
smoking in the unit and on the balcony. He testified that the Tenant has not abided by her
promise and that the smoking continues.
9. In this case, the above-summarized evidence confirms that the Tenant substantially
interfered with Ms. Petutin’s reasonable enjoyment of the residential complex for all usual
purposes by smoking on her balcony. The smoke infiltrated Ms. Petutin’s rental unit and
interfered with her breathing.
10. In these circumstances, the remaining issue is relief from eviction. This inquiry requires
me to consider the disclosed circumstances of both parties. Having done so, my finding is
that it would be unfair to grant relief from eviction pursuant to subsection 83(1) of the Act
for the following reasons. First, the Landlord was not aware of any circumstances of the
Tenant that would favour relief from eviction. Second, I agree with the Landlord that a
conditional order is not likely to be effective. The Tenant has received two N5 notices of
termination about her smoking and the Landlord has discussed the issue with the Tenant.
This ought to have put the Tenant on notice that her smoking was putting the tenancy at
risk. Although she has promised the Tenant that she would cease this conduct, she has
not kept this promise and she has continued to smoke regularly. In my view, the Tenant’s
ongoing failure to address the issue of smoking leads me to conclude that a conditional
order will not be effective to stop the Tenant from further substantial interference.
11. The Landlord collected a rent deposit of $1,554.28 from the Tenant and this deposit is still
being held by the Landlord. There is no interest owing on the rent deposit.
It is ordered that:
1. The tenancy between the Landlord and the Tenant is terminated. The Tenant must move
out of the rental unit on or before October 31, 2021.
2. The Tenant shall also pay to the Landlord $51.10 per day for compensation for the use of
the unit from October 21, 2021 to the date the Tenant moves out of the unit.
3. The Tenant shall also pay to the Landlord $186.00 for the cost of filing the application.
4. If the Tenant does not pay the Landlord the full amount owing on or before October 31,
2021, the Tenant will start to owe interest. This will be simple interest calculated from
November 1, 2021 at 2.00% annually on the balance outstanding.
5. If the unit is not vacated on or before October 31, 2021, then starting November 1, 2021,
the Landlord may file this order with the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) so that the
eviction may be enforced.
