Determinations:
INTRODUCTION
1. This application involves a newly constructed apartment in an old building that contains 5
units in total, one of which is occupied by the Landlord.
2. In January of 2017 the parties signed a fixed term lease for a six month period
commencing March 1, 2017 for a monthly rent of $1,150.00. The lawful monthly rent was
increased effective July 1, 2018 to $1,170.70. The Tenants gave notice to terminate and
moved out effective August 31, 2018.
3. The Tenants’ primary complaint is that the renovation work was incomplete when they
moved in and continued to be incomplete throughout much of the tenancy. But in
particular, the Tenants application concerns: parking; exterior walkways, steps and
railings; the dryer; a draughty exterior door; and leaking pipes. At the hearing, the
Tenants withdrew the claim with respect to leaking pipes.

Preliminary Issue
4. At the beginning of the hearing I raised a preliminary issue as to which of the named
respondents are properly named. After hearing the submissions of the parties I amended
the application. The lease is in the name of the corporation in the style of cause. In
addition, the personally named Landlord clearly meets the definition of “landlord” in the
Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the ‘Act’) as he performs all of the functions of a
landlord including renting out units and collecting the rent. But I have removed a secondnamed corporation as a party to the application as it appears to have little connection if
any to the tenancy agreement.
THE APPLICABLE LAW
5. This application is based on the rights and obligations set out in s. 20 of the Act which
says:
20 (1) A landlord is responsible for providing and maintaining a residential complex,
including the rental units in it, in a good state of repair and fit for habitation and for
complying with health, safety, housing and maintenance standards.
(2) Subsection (1) applies even if the tenant was aware of a state of non-repair or a
contravention of a standard before entering into the tenancy agreement.
6. As can be seen from this wording s, 20(1) imposes three separate obligations on a
landlord. A rental unit and residential complex must be in a good stat of repair. It also
must be fit for habitation. And finally it must comply with all relevant legal standards.
7. The Tenants’ application relies in part on the local municipality’s municipal standards bylaw which was filed into evidence.
FINDINGS
Parking
8. Parking is an issue for the residential complex and for these Tenants because between
December 1 and March 15 of every year overnight parking is forbidden on the street in
front of the building.
9. The residential complex has parking spaces available in both the front and the back. At
the back there are four spaces which the Landlord may expand into five. At the front there
are three paved spots and a fourth unpaved spot. The parking spaces at the back are
reached by a municipally owned access road. Both the parking area in the back and that
access road are unpaved. Pictures show that the area is packed earth.
10. Although the Tenants’ application says the Tenants’ lease gave them three parking
spaces, the written lease actually does not say that. Rather it says parking is included
with the rent and the actual parking is “To be allocated.”

11. But there is no dispute that the Tenants told the Landlord they would need parking for two
vehicles and for three seasons of the year, a motorcycle. On January 29, 2017 the
second-named Tenant sent the Landlord an e-mail saying the Tenants wanted to rent the
unit and:
We would have one vehicle for now but a second by spring but a motorcycle then as
well. My husband would want to park the motorcycle in front so would we have
space for our other 2 vehicles in the back?
12. To which the Landlord wrote back:
Yes there is room there. I have a little landscaping to do in the spring plus there is
two mini vans there (old work vehicles of my company) that I will deal with by then.
A motorbike in the front wont be any issue.
13. Unfortunately, the Landlord did not follow through with this planned work prior to the
Tenants moving in on March 1, 2017. At that time they had only one vehicle, the second
was acquired shortly afterwards, and the motorcycle was in storage.
14. The first problem was that some of the spaces in back were still occupied by the
Landlord’s vehicles, and what sounds like construction debris. Other spots were occupied
by other tenants, so the Tenants could not park in back as planned. The Landlord’s
vehicles and debris remained in back until November of 2017.
15. Another problem with parking in back was the landscaping had not been done. In order to
get to the parking area in back the Tenants needed to go along the crushed stone
pathway and down a lawn. At the bottom of the lawn was a slope that can only be
navigated by steps which were either broken or missing. And the surface was unpaved.
16. So initially on move in the Tenants parked their one vehicle in front. When they acquired
the second sometime between late May and July of 2017 it was after March 15 so they
could park that one on the street for a while.
17. Unfortunately the spot in front had an unfinished surface. From the photographs filed it
looks like it was originally lawn. When the Tenants’ motorcycle came out of storage in
June of 2017 they wanted to park it off the street but needed a better surface to do so. So
in late July of 2017 the Landlord placed three patio stones down in that area for the
motorcycle to sit on. The Tenants continued to park their other vehicles on the street.
18. Then in November of 2017 the Landlord removed some of the construction debris and
vehicles in back, so one of the Tenants could use a space in the back and did so. The
second named Tenant above continued to park in front where the motorcycle had been
during the riding season. But over the winter of 2017 to 2018 the front parking spot
became unusable because of the muddy surface. Her vehicle got stuck. As a result of
that, the second named Tenant parked her car on the street overnight and was ticketed.
The Landlord offered his own spot for days when he was out of town but the Tenants
received other tickets during the period December 1, 2017 to March 15, 2018. The
Tenants say those tickets cost them $40.00.
