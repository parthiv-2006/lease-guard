BACKGROUND

The Landlord applied for termination of the tenancy under section 48 of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 on the basis that the Landlord required the rental unit for the Landlord's own use. The Landlord served an N12 notice with a termination date of September 30, 2022, providing more than 60 days' notice. The Landlord stated the unit was needed for the Landlord's adult daughter who recently moved back to the Toronto area.

The Tenant contested the notice, alleging bad faith and noting that the unit was listed for rent at a higher price on a rental website within two weeks of the notice being served.

ANALYSIS

Section 48(1) of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 permits a landlord to terminate a tenancy if the landlord in good faith requires the rental unit for residential occupation by a specified family member, including a child. The requirement of good faith is essential — the landlord must genuinely intend for the named person to occupy the unit.

The Tenant produced a screenshot of a rental listing for the unit dated September 17, 2022 — just 13 days after the N12 notice was served. The listed rent was $600 per month more than the current Tenant's rent. The Landlord did not provide a satisfactory explanation for this listing.

Section 57 of the RTA provides that a former tenant may apply if the landlord gave a notice in bad faith. The listing of the unit at a higher rent so shortly after serving the N12 notice is strong evidence of bad faith. The Landlord's testimony that the daughter ultimately decided not to move in — without explanation — further undermines the claim of genuine need.

I find on the balance of probabilities that the N12 notice was served in bad faith. The Landlord's application is dismissed.

ORDER

1. The Landlord's application is dismissed.
2. The tenancy shall continue.
