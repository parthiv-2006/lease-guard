PreliminaryIssue
**
1. The Tenant, Gloria
Mendes (GM), submitted that Cristal Mendes (CM) should not be apartytothisapplication.

2. GM testified that CM

testifiedthatonlyCMhadsignedthenotice.

3. Cristal Mendes
submitted that she should be removed from the proceedings because shewasnot
intheunitatthetimetheapplicationwasfiled.
CMtestifiedthat shedidnotfollow
up with the Landlord with respect to the N9 notice because Doug, thesuperintendent, told her that he had
received the notice in time, therefore she would beable to move out February 28, 2019. CM also testified that she
stayed with her eldestsister when
she vacated, and she had a witness who could corroborate that she hadvacated when she said. CM did not have a
copy of the N9, and testified that she did notunderstand the concept of a joint tenancy and that it required
permission from theLandlord toberemoved.
4. I advised CM that it
was not necessary to hear witness testimony as it is not disputed thatshe vacated,the issueiswhethersheisliableforthearrears.
5. Erin Richardson (ER),
on behalf of the Landlord, testified that the first she became awarethat CM was no longer living in the unit
was at approximately 1:20 p.m. on the day of thehearing. She also testified that GM never mentioned to her that
CM was no longer in theunit when
they discussed repayment options. Further, ER, testified that all N9s are kepton file, and she does not have one from
CM. She did not receive a N9 from Doug anddoesnotbelieve
he would havesaid he accepted itbecausethatisnot his role.
6. ER also testified that
in the case of a joint tenancy where the tenants pay separately, it isnoted on the ledger the amount paid and by
whom. To the best of her knowledge, up untilNovember2020the rent waspaid intwopayments, onefromeachof the Tenants.
7. The Landlord’s Legal
Representative submitted that both Tenant’s should be held liablefor thearrears asthisisajointtenancy.Further,itisimportanttonotethattheN9wasnot
put into evidence, and neither GM nor CM followed up with the Landlord. Not
knowingthe law is not a defense, to
be removed from a joint tenancy required the Landlord’sconsent.
8. ForthereasonsthatfollowCristalMendesremainsapartytothisapplication.
9. In a joint tenancy,
there is a single tenancy agreement and the tenants are jointly andseverally, meaning together and
individually, liable for the payment of the entire rent forthe rental unit. Effectively this means
that where there is a joint tenancy between two ormoretenantsandarrearsareowingtothelandlord,eachtenantcanbeheldliablefortheentirearrears.
10.In a number of recent decisions, the Board
has found that to terminate a joint tenancy, allthe tenants as well as the landlord must agree to the
termination.In my view, this is thecorrect approach. This tenancy was formed
by a three-party agreement between GM,CM,
and the Landlord.The agreement of
all three parties would also be required todissolve
either all or part of it. CM signing a N9 notice of termination was a
unilateral act.For it to have been
proper notice would have required GM notifying the Landlord that sheconsentedtoCM’s removal,plustheLandlordhadtoagree.
11.Further, neither Tenant made an attempt to
ensure that CM had been removed from thetenancy
agreement, even though they never received anything in writing from theLandlord with respect to having received
the N9 notice. Logically, a landlord wouldrespond
in writing to confirm either acceptance or rejection of such a notice. It was
naïveof the Tenants to assume that
CM’s portion of the tenancy was terminated; they shouldhave soughtconfirmationfromtheLandlord.
12.Therefore, as CM was not properly removed
from the lease, her portion of the tenancywas
not terminated, and she remains liable under the terms of the tenancy until
such timeasthepropersteps aretaken toterminateherinterest.
