BACKGROUND

The Tenant applied regarding a key deposit and security deposit. The lease required a $500 key deposit and a $2,200 "security deposit for damage." The monthly rent was $2,100. The Tenant paid both amounts prior to moving in.

ANALYSIS

Section 105(1) of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 permits only a rent deposit not exceeding one month's rent, to be applied toward the last rental period. Section 106(1) permits a key deposit not exceeding the actual cost to replace the keys. A general damage deposit or security deposit is not authorized under the Act.

The $500 key deposit: The Landlord produced evidence that three key fobs were provided, each costing $85 to replace, for a total of $255. A key deposit under section 106(1) may only equal the landlord's reasonable cost to replace the keys. The excess of $245 was illegally collected.

The $2,200 security deposit: This is entirely illegal. There is no provision in the Act permitting a landlord to collect a damage or security deposit. Section 134(1) expressly prohibits collection of any amount beyond what the Act permits. The full $2,200 must be returned.

The Landlord shall repay $2,200.00 for the illegal security deposit and $245.00 for the excess key deposit, totalling $2,445.00.

ORDER

1. The application is granted.
2. The Landlord shall pay the Tenant $2,445.00, being $2,200.00 for the illegal security deposit and $245.00 for the portion of the key deposit exceeding the actual replacement cost, both collected in violation of sections 105, 106, and 134 of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006.
3. The Landlord shall pay the Tenant $48.00 for the cost of filing the application.
