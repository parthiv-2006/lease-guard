ONTARIO LANDLORD AND TENANT BOARD
File Number: TNT-50001-23

BETWEEN:
Applicant Tenant
and
Respondent Landlord

REASONS FOR ORDER

BACKGROUND
The Tenant applied after the Landlord repeatedly entered the rental unit without providing the 24-hour written notice required by the Act, relying on a lease clause that purported to reduce the required notice period to four hours and to permit entry for inspections at the Landlord's "reasonable discretion."

ANALYSIS
The tenancy agreement stated: "The Landlord may enter the rental unit upon providing four (4) hours' verbal or written notice for the purposes of inspection, repairs, or showing the unit to prospective tenants or purchasers. The Tenant consents to entry upon this reduced notice."

Section 27(1) of the Act requires a landlord to give at least 24 hours' written notice before entering a rental unit, specifying the reason for entry and the time of entry (between 8 a.m. and 8 p.m.). Section 26(1) limits the reasons for entry to those specified in the Act.

A clause reducing the notice period from 24 hours to 4 hours is inconsistent with section 27 and is void under section 3. The tenant's apparent consent to reduced notice in the lease does not save the clause — section 3 makes void any agreement that provides a right of entry inconsistent with the Act, and the tenant's contractual consent cannot override a statutory minimum.

The Board notes the importance of this protection. A tenant's home is entitled to privacy. The 24-hour written notice requirement is not a procedural technicality but a substantive privacy protection.

PRINCIPLE
A clause purporting to reduce the landlord's entry notice obligation below 24 hours, or permitting verbal-only notice, or granting entry at the landlord's discretion beyond the Act's specified purposes, is void under sections 3 and 27 of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006. The 24-hour written notice requirement is a minimum that cannot be contracted away.

ORDER
The Board finds the Landlord substantially interfered with the Tenant's reasonable enjoyment. A remedy of $500 is awarded to the Tenant.
